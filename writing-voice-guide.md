# GERA YEREMIN'S WRITING VOICE GUIDE

*Last Updated: November 22, 2025*

---

## Purpose

This guide defines Gera Yeremin's authentic writing voice to ensure all content sounds natural and genuine while maintaining SEO effectiveness. Use this for blog posts, emails, social media, and any client-facing content.

---

## CORE PRINCIPLE: Direct, Conversational, No BS

Gera's voice is **professional without being corporate**. Think: experienced developer talking to a business owner at a coffee shop, not a consultant pitching in a boardroom.

---

## Opening Styles

### **Professional/Business Contexts (Blog Posts, Landing Pages)**

- **Direct, casual professional:** "Hey [Name]," or jump straight to the point
- **Action-oriented:** Lead with the main point quickly
- **Problem-focused:** When addressing issues, get straight to the context

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Your team is drowning in spreadsheets."
- ✅ "Let's talk about what custom software actually costs."
- ❌ "In today's rapidly evolving business landscape, organizations are increasingly..."

### **Casual/Familiar Contexts (Social Media, Email)**

- Very casual, minimal punctuation
- Direct address with first names only
- Extremely brief responses when appropriate

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Ya we ate them. Was something wrong with them?"
- ✅ "Hey John, just wanted to check in on this."
- ❌ "Dear valued colleague, I hope this message finds you well..."

---

## Structural Patterns

### **Short-form responses (Social Media, Quick Emails)**
- Single sentences to 2-3 sentences max when appropriate
- No fluff, just the answer

**Example:**
- ✅ "Ya we ate them. Was something wrong with them?"
- ❌ "Thank you so much for reaching out! I really appreciate your message. Yes, we did consume them. I was wondering if perhaps there might have been an issue?"

### **Medium-form messages (Standard Business Emails)**
- 2-4 sentences with specific information
- States current situation directly
- Provides context or reasoning
- Includes actionable next steps

**Example:**
- ✅ "So, currently, there are two pictures of Anya in the system, an older one and a newer one. I can update that if you need. Let me know."
- ❌ "I am writing to inform you that upon reviewing the system, I have discovered that there exist two photographs..."

### **Longer professional content (Blog Posts, Guides)**
- Multi-paragraph structure when explaining complex topics
- Opening statement of situation/problem
- Clarification and context
- Technical details or reasoning
- Next steps or call to action
- **But never academic or overly formal**

---

## Tone Variations by Context

### **Educational Content (Blog Posts, Guides)**
- Casual but authoritative
- Uses questions to engage reader: "Sound familiar?"
- Shows understanding of their pain points
- Problem-solving orientation
- Conversational even when technical

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Here's the truth: custom software isn't for everyone."
- ✅ "You've looked at off-the-shelf software. Nothing quite fits."
- ❌ "It is important to note that custom software solutions may not be appropriate for all organizations."

### **Business/Sales Context (Landing Pages, Service Pages)**
- More structured but still conversational
- Direct and concise
- States value clearly
- Action-oriented with clear next steps
- No sales-y language or hype

**Examples:**
- ✅ "I build custom software for businesses that have outgrown off-the-shelf solutions."
- ✅ "My goal is to build something that actually makes you money."
- ❌ "Leverage our cutting-edge solutions to maximize your ROI and revolutionize your business processes!"

### **Technical Explanations (Case Studies, Dev Blogs)**
- Uses specific technical terms naturally
- Explains "why" behind decisions
- Links to resources when helpful
- States limitations or current state before proposing solutions
- Never talks down to reader

**Examples:**
- ✅ "The first roadblock I hit was figuring out what compliance is required."
- ✅ "You would need to integrate with their API, which has some quirks."
- ❌ "One must first ascertain the regulatory requirements before proceeding with implementation."

## Vocabulary & Phrasing

### **Characteristic Phrases**
- "Just wanted to..." (for check-ins)
- "My goal is to..." (when explaining business purpose)
- "If you have [any questions/concerns]..." (inviting feedback)
- "Let me know" (action request)
- "Here's the truth..." (before honest assessment)
- "Sound familiar?" (relating to reader's experience)
- "The reality..." (when being frank)
- Direct contractions: "I've," "you're," "it's," "don't"

### **Avoid These Phrases**
- ❌ "Leverage synergies"
- ❌ "Best-in-class solutions"
- ❌ "Revolutionize your business"
- ❌ "I hope this email finds you well"
- ❌ "Moving forward"
- ❌ "Paradigm shift"
- ❌ "Circle back"
- ❌ "Touch base"

### **Technical Communication**
- Uses specific product names and technical terms naturally
- Links resources when helpful
- States limitations or current state before proposing solutions
- Explains "why" behind technical decisions
- Never assumes reader knows everything, but doesn't condescend

**Examples:**
- ✅ "I use React for most projects because it's what I know best and the ecosystem is mature."
- ✅ "You'll need HIPAA compliance, which adds about $15K to the project."
- ❌ "Utilizing a modern JavaScript framework such as React enables the development of highly performant user interfaces."

## Sentence Structure

### **Length & Flow**
- Generally medium-length sentences (15-20 words average)
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
- Uses dashes for clarification or additional thought
- Rhetorical questions to engage reader: "Sound familiar?" "What if there was another way?"
- Rarely uses exclamation marks; prefers periods even in positive messages
- Direct, active voice preferred

### **Examples**

**✅ Good:**
- "Your team is drowning in spreadsheets. Your sales data lives in one system, customer info in another, and your unique workflow? It's held together with duct tape."
- "The first roadblock I hit was figuring out compliance. You would need to contact Telnyx support."

**❌ Avoid:**
- "Your organizational team is currently experiencing significant challenges with regard to spreadsheet management!"
- "Upon encountering the initial obstacle, which pertained to regulatory compliance matters, it became necessary to engage with the technical support division."

---

## Paragraph Structure

### **Opening Paragraphs**
- Hook with a relatable problem or surprising truth
- Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max)
- Get to the point fast

**✅ Good:**
```
Your team is drowning in spreadsheets.

Sound familiar? You've looked at off-the-shelf software. Nothing quite fits.

What if there was another way?
```

**❌ Avoid:**
```
In today's rapidly evolving business landscape, organizations of all sizes are increasingly recognizing the critical importance of effective data management solutions. Many companies find themselves facing significant challenges when attempting to manage their business processes using traditional spreadsheet applications.
```

### **Body Paragraphs**
- One idea per paragraph
- Short paragraphs for readability (especially online)
- Use subheadings liberally
- Break up text with lists, examples, and white space

### **Transitional Phrases**
- "Here's the thing..."
- "The reality is..."
- "Let's talk about..."
- "Now here's where it gets interesting..."
- "But wait..."

## DO's and DON'ts Summary

### **DO:**
- ✅ Be direct and get to the point quickly
- ✅ Ask questions to understand/engage reader
- ✅ Provide context before solutions
- ✅ Use casual language even in professional contexts
- ✅ Include relevant links or resources
- ✅ Show genuine understanding of reader's pain points
- ✅ Use "you" and "I" liberally (never "we" unless referring to a team)
- ✅ Admit limitations or unknowns honestly
- ✅ Give real numbers, real examples
- ✅ Use contractions naturally
- ✅ Write like you talk

### **DON'T:**
- ❌ Use overly formal business language
- ❌ Bury the main point in lengthy preamble
- ❌ Use excessive punctuation or emojis (occasional emoji okay, not every sentence)
- ❌ Write longer than necessary
- ❌ Use jargon without explanation
- ❌ Include unnecessary pleasantries
- ❌ Use corporate buzzwords
- ❌ Make grandiose promises
- ❌ Talk about "we" when it's just you
- ❌ Use passive voice unnecessarily
- ❌ Say "solution" when you mean "software" or "tool"

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### **Mistake #1: Being Too Casual**

**❌ Too casual:**
- "lol yeah custom software is dope, hmu if u need it"

**✅ Right level:**
- "Custom software might be the answer you've been looking for. Let's talk about whether it makes sense for your business."

### **Mistake #2: Being Too Formal**

**❌ Too formal:**
- "We cordially invite you to schedule a consultation to discuss your custom software requirements."

**✅ Right level:**
- "Want to discuss your specific situation? Schedule a free 30-minute call."

### **Mistake #3: Overselling**

**❌ Hype:**
- "Our revolutionary custom software solutions will transform your business and skyrocket your ROI!"

**✅ Honest:**
- "Custom software can give you a competitive edge. But it's not right for everyone. Here's how to know if it makes sense for you."

### **Mistake #4: Underselling**

**❌ Too humble:**
- "I might be able to help with some basic software stuff if you want..."

**✅ Confident:**
- "I build custom software for businesses making $500K-$5M/year. Projects typically range from $15K-$150K."
